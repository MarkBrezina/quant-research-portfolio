
## Recent Explorations in Market Microstructure

My recent research has focused on market microstructure, with particular attention to how short-horizon price formation emerges from the interaction between order book state, order flow, and liquidity changes. A central theme in this work has been the idea that price does not respond only to the visible top-of-book imbalance, but to a broader and more persistent supply-demand structure built from the evolving memory of orders resting in the book.

A major part of this exploration has involved constructing richer order imbalance measures. Rather than relying only on the first few displayed levels, I have been experimenting with engines that track a deeper and more persistent representation of the order book, allowing past orders to remain in memory until they are filled or cancelled. This produces a less noisy and, in many cases, more informative view of latent pressure on the bid and ask sides. The goal has been to better capture where genuine liquidity sits, how it changes over time, and how that relates to near-term directional movement.

Alongside this, I have been analysing order flow in a more disaggregated way by separating bid-side and ask-side additions, cancellations, and trade-related flow. This makes it possible to distinguish between different mechanisms of pressure: aggressive trading, passive liquidity provision, and liquidity withdrawal. In practice, these dynamics often matter as much as the static snapshot of the book itself. A market can look balanced on the surface while still carrying directional pressure through persistent cancellations on one side or steady replenishment on the other.

A further research direction has been the search for equilibrium points between imbalance and flow. Conceptually, I have been exploring whether there exists a local supply-demand equilibrium around which price fluctuates, and whether larger deviations from that equilibrium are associated with stronger drift, trend continuation, or reversion. This has opened up questions around whether equilibrium should be treated as fixed, state-dependent, or horizon-dependent, especially since short-term microstructure balance may differ materially from longer-term liquidity pressure.

Methodologically, this work has combined feature design, rolling aggregation, state tracking, and exploratory statistical testing. I have been testing how imbalance behaves when grouped in event time versus clock time, how rolling sums compare with rolling means, how weighting by level changes predictive power, and how volume-weighted prices can be used to summarize the effective location of liquidity in the book. I have also been examining how these features interact with realized price changes to identify which combinations appear to carry real explanatory value.

Overall, this line of research is aimed at building a more realistic view of intraday price formation: one where price evolves not simply from trades alone, but from the full interaction of displayed liquidity, hidden persistence, replenishment, cancellation, and flow imbalance. The broader objective is to turn these observations into robust microstructure signals that can later support execution models, short-horizon forecasting, and alpha generation.
