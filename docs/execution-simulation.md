## Execution Simulation and Market Simulation Research

A recent direction in my research has been the development of execution and market simulation frameworks aimed at better understanding how prices emerge from the interaction of heterogeneous market participants. This work is motivated by the idea that market behaviour is not well described by price series alone, but instead arises from the evolving interaction between liquidity provision, order submission, execution, inventory management, and agent response. That perspective is closely aligned with agent-based computational finance, where heterogeneous investors and trading rules are used to reproduce market dynamics and microstructure effects.

In my current notebook-based work, I have been exploring the foundations of an artificial market in Python, with the intention of extending it into a richer multi-agent environment. The next step is to simulate a broader ecology of participants, including market-makers, retail investors, systematic investors, large institutional investors, and algorithmic traders, each with different objectives, constraints, and behavioural rules. This direction is well supported by the broader literature, where agent-based market models are used precisely because they can represent heterogeneous investors, trading environments, and microstructural interactions more realistically than purely aggregate models.

A key part of the planned development is to make these agents more realistic by giving them memory, preferences, resistance to changing positions, and behavioural or psychological tendencies. In practical terms, this means moving beyond simple reactive rules toward agents that adapt to past outcomes, process information differently, and respond asymmetrically to gains, losses, liquidity conditions, and volatility. Research in agent-based finance has repeatedly emphasized that bounded rationality, learning, and heterogeneous behaviour are central to explaining emergent phenomena such as clustering, instability, persistent volatility, and changing market regimes.

The broader objective of this research is not just to generate synthetic prices, but to build a simulation environment that can be used to study execution quality, market impact, liquidity dynamics, and the structural mechanics of trading. Agent-based liquidity and microstructure models are increasingly used for this kind of purpose, including the study of transaction costs, large-order execution, market design, and rule changes such as tick-size adjustments or settlement protocols.

An important future layer of this work is visualization. I want to accompany the simulations with animations that make the mechanics of the market easier to interpret: how orders enter and leave the book, how liquidity shifts across the bid and ask, how different agent types interact, and how local imbalances can grow into broader price movements. That kind of visual approach is especially useful in market simulation research because the value of the model often lies in understanding emergent behaviour, not just final summary statistics.
